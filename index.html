Hi, I’m Andrea Loya Castillo, a Ph.D. student in the Department of Organismic and Evolutionary Biology at Harvard University, where I work in Ben de Bivort’s lab. My research explores how insect behavior evolves across species and environments. Starting at the the nuanced steps of individual insects to broad ecological patterns visible from space. I’m fascinated by how behavior encodes evolutionary history and environmental change, and I aim to connect scales of observation — from the microstructure of movement to the macroecology of landscapes shaped by fire, climate, and time.

I grew up in California surrounded by landscapes that constantly changed — places where wildfire, drought, and regeneration were part of the natural rhythm. That experience sparked a lifelong curiosity about how organisms adapt to disturbance and how resilience is expressed not just in genes or ecosystems, but in behavior itself. Before joining Harvard, I worked on insect biodiversity and wildfire ecology, using satellite imagery to map post-fire recovery and quantify how disturbance reshapes ecosystems. Those early projects taught me how data, when used creatively, can help us see life from new perspectives.

Now, my work sits at the intersection of behavioral evolution, ecology, and machine learning. I’m building tools — both mechanical and computational — to study how insects move, adapt, and persist in changing environments. This includes curating cross-species behavioral datasets and integrating remote sensing to link animal behavior with environmental gradients. Outside of research, I love reading, being outdoors, and spending time with friends. Whether I’m hiking in the woods, spending time at the beach, curled up with a book, or getting into (mostly harmless) trouble with my favorite people, I find joy in curiosity, collaboration, and the spaces between them. Hopefullu I can bring that same curiosity and sense of joy into my work.

Through my research, I hope to uncover how behavior reflects both evolutionary history and present-day environments. My long-term goal is to build frameworks that connect behavioral diversity to ecological resilience, helping us understand not just how species survive change, but how they adapt and thrive within it.

Beyond research, my personal goal is to leave the world a little better than I found it — through mentorship, education, and curiosity shared freely. I believe science should be generous: it should inspire, include, and empower others to see themselves as part of the process of discovery. Whether through teaching, collaboration, or community work, I hope to help make knowledge feel like something that belongs to everyone.
